Salut, Cher!

It would be impossible to understand my relationship with customer satisfaction without recognizing the place where it's almost a way of life, my hometown, New Orleans. In southern Louisiana, there's a cultural mindset that has helped it weather every storm that has washed through: "When you take care of your community, it takes care of you in return."

Over the generations, that has grown into one word, which any local can tell you that everybody needs... Lagniappe.

Lann-Yapp

If you've never heard the word, I wouldn't blame you for swearing I just made that up. But if you give me 60 seconds, I'll change your world.

Lagniappe is a word that was given to french creoles of the Mississippi River delta, which incidently translates to "a little something extra, given in good faith." Traditionally, it represented the 13th roll in a baker's dozen or a spritz of cologne to go with your shoe shine.

Bless Yo Heart

Of course, it's not really a radical idea. However, it's incredible how effective it can be across both business and personal matters. Just a little extra time can flip around a stranger's entire day. A little extra effort can sweeten a sour mother-in-law; that one is tried and tested. And you'd be surprised how much a "little" extra money can mean from another's perspective.

It doesn't usually require much, but once you meet the need, everybody could use a little lagniappe.

So, Why Raisely?

My last five years have included an acquisition, an IPO, and the ability to bridge my customer service experience into software engineering. In my next five years, I'd like to see that experience grow into a junior developer role, and finally a senior developer role.

Raisely is in a unique position to allow me to provide value to the company based on both my personal and professional experience, while growing my knowledge as a developer. The sense of community that I've felt by researching the company online simply verifies that Raisely shares the same values I do.

My Platform Crush

And finally, I really admire my former employers, hack.hands(), for their solution to connect inexperienced developers who don't understand their projects' bugs with more seasoned engineers in the market for on-demand work.
